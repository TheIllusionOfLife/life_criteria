\title{Searching for an Eighth Criterion of Life:\\
A Falsifiable Framework and Two Null Results}

\author{Anonymous}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
The textbook seven criteria of life---cellular organization, metabolism,
homeostasis, growth, reproduction, response to stimuli, and
evolution---are widely used to characterize living systems, yet it
remains unclear whether they suffice to explain resilience under novel
perturbations.  We present a falsifiable framework for evaluating
candidate eighth criteria, grounded in functional analogy: each
criterion must be a dynamic, energy-consuming process whose ablation
causes measurable degradation and whose activity forms feedback loops
with other criteria.  We define three legitimacy tests---orthogonality,
non-reducibility, and causal necessity---and validate the framework's
diagnostic power by confirming that all seven existing criteria produce
large ablation effects ($d = 0.78$--$14.7$, all $p < 0.005$).
We then test two candidates under famine and boom-bust perturbation
regimes ($n=30$ seeds per condition, held-out test set, paired
Wilcoxon signed-rank with Holm--Bonferroni correction):
\textbf{Candidate~A} (within-lifetime learning via EMA-driven
homeostatic correction) and \textbf{Candidate~B} (collective
organization via kin-sensing).  Both yield null results: all paired
$|d_z| < 0.33$, all adjusted $p \geq 0.19$, with formal TOST
equivalence testing confirming several comparisons fall within our
pre-specified SESOI of $\pm 0.5$.  Exploratory legitimacy tests confirm
neither candidate adds incremental predictive validity
($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$, permutation $p > 0.37$) and both candidate
signals are reducible to existing criteria (cross-validated $R^2 > 0.88$).
These bounded null results constrain each candidate's effect below
practical significance, while the validated protocol provides a reusable
template for future candidate evaluation.  Code and data:
\url{https://github.com/TheIllusionOfLife/life_criteria}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

What makes a system life-like?  Introductory biology textbooks
commonly enumerate seven criteria: cellular organization, metabolism,
homeostasis, growth and development, reproduction, response to stimuli,
and evolution \citep{urry_2020_campbell}.  These criteria serve as a
practical checklist, yet whether they constitute a \emph{sufficient}
characterization of life---especially under novel or extreme
conditions---remains an open question
\citep{cleland_2002_defining, benner_2010_defining}.

Artificial life research has long sought to operationalize these
criteria computationally.  Systems such as Tierra \citep{ray_1991_approach},
Avida \citep{ofria_2004_avida}, Polyworld \citep{yaeger_1994_computational},
Lenia \citep{chan_2019_lenia}, and ALIEN \citep{heinemann_2008_alien}
each implement subsets of life-like properties, from self-replication and
open-ended evolution to continuous morphogenesis.  Recent work on
Flow-Lenia \citep{plantec_2023_flow} and Coralai
\citep{barbieux_2024_coralai} pushes toward mass-conserving,
embodied neural ecosystems.  Theoretical frameworks from autopoiesis
\citep{maturana_1980_autopoiesis} through chemoton theory
\citep{ganti_2003_principles} emphasize the integration of metabolic,
boundary, and informational subsystems as minimal requirements
\citep{ruizmirazo_2004_universal}.

Despite this rich landscape, few systems implement \emph{all seven}
criteria as interdependent, individually ablatable processes---and
fewer still ask whether the seven are \emph{sufficient}.
\citet{bedau_2000_open} identified open problems in ALife that implicitly
point beyond the seven, including the evolution of adaptive behavior
and the emergence of collective organization.
The question we address is methodological: \emph{how should one
rigorously test whether an additional criterion provides explanatory
power beyond the existing seven?}

We contribute:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A \textbf{falsifiable evaluation protocol} for candidate eighth
  criteria, with three legitimacy tests (orthogonality, non-reducibility,
  causal necessity), paired statistical analysis, and formal equivalence
  testing (TOST).
\item \textbf{Framework validation}: all seven existing criteria produce
  large, significant ablation effects ($d = 0.78$--$14.7$), confirming
  the system's diagnostic power before testing new candidates.
\item \textbf{Rigorous null results} for two candidates---within-lifetime
  learning/memory and collective kin-sensing---each tested under two
  perturbation regimes with $n=30$ seeds, four conditions, and
  Holm--Bonferroni correction.
\item \textbf{Exploratory legitimacy tests}: both candidates fail
  orthogonality ($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$) and non-reducibility
  (cross-validated $R^2 > 0.88$) analyses, confirming neither adds
  genuinely new information.
\item \textbf{Mechanistic diagnoses} explaining each null: the memory
  mechanism works but adds no survival advantage; the kin signal becomes
  degenerate under the tested demographic regime.
\end{enumerate}

Reporting null results is essential for cumulative science
\citep{rosenthal_1979_file, fanelli_2012_negative}.  By defining our
smallest effect size of interest (SESOI) \emph{a priori} and applying
formal equivalence testing \citep{lakens_2017_equivalence,
lakens_2018_sesoi}, we transform ``failure to reject $H_0$'' into
bounded null results.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{System Architecture}

Our simulator implements a hybrid two-layer architecture.  At the lower
level, \textbf{swarm agents} (10--50 per organism) inhabit a continuous
2D environment and maintain organism cohesion through local coordination.
At the upper level, \textbf{organisms} (10--50 per simulation) emerge
from coordinated agent groups and possess individual genomes, neural
controllers, and metabolic state.

Each of the seven biological criteria is realized as a \emph{functional
analogy}: a computational process that (a)~is a dynamic process
requiring sustained resource consumption, (b)~whose removal causes
measurable degradation of organism self-maintenance, and (c)~forms
feedback loops with at least one other criterion.  The seven criteria
map to specific subsystems:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Cellular organization}: Active boundary maintenance via
  swarm coordination; degrades without energy.
\item \textbf{Metabolism}: Graph-based multi-step transformation
  network converting environmental resources to internal energy.
\item \textbf{Homeostasis}: Neural network controller regulates an
  internal state vector within viable ranges.
\item \textbf{Growth/Development}: Minimal seed state develops into
  mature organism via genetically encoded developmental program.
\item \textbf{Reproduction}: Organism-initiated division when
  metabolically ready; offspring develop from seed.
\item \textbf{Response to stimuli}: Local sensory field processed by
  neural network; emergent behavioral repertoire.
\item \textbf{Evolution}: Heritable genomes with mutation and
  recombination; differential survival drives adaptation.
\end{enumerate}

All organisms share a fixed-topology neural controller
($8 \to 16 \to 4$, 212 weights) with genome-encoded weights evolved
through tournament selection.  Key simulation parameters are listed in
Table~\ref{tab:params}.  Simulations run for 10{,}000 timesteps
with deterministic seeding (same seed $+$ config $\to$ identical
output).


\subsection{Seven-Criteria Validation}

Before testing new candidates, we validated the framework's diagnostic
power by ablating each of the seven criteria individually ($n = 30$
seeds, 2{,}000 steps, graph-based metabolism).
Figure~\ref{fig:7criteria} shows the results: all seven ablations
produce significant effects (Table~\ref{tab:7criteria}), with
Cohen's~$d$ ranging from 0.78 (evolution) to 14.7 (reproduction).
This confirms that the simulator implements each criterion as a
genuinely functional process whose removal causes measurable
degradation---a prerequisite for meaningful eighth-criterion testing.


\subsection{Extending to an Eighth Criterion}

To avoid the ``arbitrary add-on'' criticism, we require any candidate
eighth criterion to satisfy the same functional analogy conditions
(a)--(c) plus an additional condition specific to the candidate's
domain.  We define three \textbf{legitimacy tests}:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Orthogonality} (incremental predictive validity): The
  candidate indicator must add $R^2$ to a regression predicting survival
  AUC beyond seven-criteria proxy signals.
\item \textbf{Non-reducibility}: The candidate-specific signal cannot be
  predicted from the seven-criteria proxies alone (low cross-validated
  $R^2$).
\item \textbf{Causal necessity}: Ablating the candidate causes
  measurable degradation under appropriate conditions.
\end{enumerate}

A candidate that fails any legitimacy test under a given regime receives
a \emph{regime-specific null}, not a universal rejection---the
candidate may succeed under different ecological conditions.


\subsection{Experimental Design}

For each candidate $\times$ perturbation regime, we run four conditions
on $n = 30$ held-out seeds (seeds 100--129, disjoint from the
calibration set 0--99):
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Baseline}: Seven criteria active, candidate inactive.
\item \textbf{Candidate enabled}: Candidate mechanism active.
\item \textbf{Candidate ablated}: Candidate mechanism zeroed/disabled,
  everything else identical to the enabled condition.
\item \textbf{Sham control}: Compute-matched random updates replacing
  the candidate's actual mechanism.
\end{enumerate}

Two perturbation regimes stress the system beyond normal operating
conditions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Famine}: Resource availability drops to near-zero at
  step~3{,}000, creating acute survival pressure.
\item \textbf{Boom-bust}: Cyclic resource availability with period
  2{,}500 steps, creating recurring bottlenecks.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Statistical pipeline.}
Because all four conditions share the same 30 seeds, yielding paired
observations, our primary analysis uses the \textbf{Wilcoxon signed-rank
test} \citep{wilcoxon_1945_individual} on seed-matched AUC differences.
Holm--Bonferroni correction \citep{holm_1979_simple} is applied within
each candidate $\times$ regime family (3 comparisons per family: enabled,
ablated, and sham, each vs.\ baseline).  Effect sizes are reported as
paired Cohen's~$d_z$ (mean difference divided by standard deviation of
differences) with 95\% confidence intervals, and Cliff's~$\delta$
\citep{cliff_1993_dominance} as a nonparametric complement.
Mann--Whitney $U$ \citep{mann_1947_test} results are reported as
secondary (unpaired) analyses for comparison with prior work.

\paragraph{Equivalence testing.}
We pre-specify a smallest effect size of interest (SESOI) of $|d| = 0.5$
(medium effect per Cohen's conventions) and apply \textbf{Two One-Sided
Tests} (TOST; \citealt{lakens_2017_equivalence}) against bounds
$\pm 0.5$.  A comparison is considered equivalent to null when
$\text{TOST}\ p < 0.05$, meaning the 90\% CI for $d_z$ falls entirely
within $[-0.5, 0.5]$.

\paragraph{Analysis plan.}
Primary analyses (survival AUC comparisons) use Holm--Bonferroni
correction.  Exploratory analyses (orthogonality regression,
non-reducibility, pre-shock window) are reported as exploratory with
permutation-based $p$-values but without family-wise correction.

\section{Candidate A: Within-Lifetime Memory}

\subsection{Implementation}

Candidate~A adds a \textbf{memory-driven homeostatic correction phase}
operating alongside (but separately from) the neural controller.  Each
organism maintains a 2-element exponential moving average (EMA) tracking
the mean internal state of its agents:
\begin{equation}
m_{t+1}^{(i)} = \alpha \cdot m_t^{(i)} + (1 - \alpha) \cdot
\bar{s}_t^{(i)}, \quad i \in \{0, 1\}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{s}_t^{(i)}$ is the mean of internal-state channel~$i$ across
the organism's agents, and $\alpha \in [0, 1]$ is a configurable decay
parameter.  The memory trace drives a proportional correction toward a
genome-modulated target: four genome-encoded parameters (two gains, two
targets) control the strength and set-point of the correction, which is
applied directly to agent internal states each timestep.  Memory is
initialized to zero at birth.  The neural controller architecture
(212~weights for the base 8-input topology) is unchanged; the memory
phase adds only four evolvable genome parameters.

This design satisfies the functional analogy conditions: it is a dynamic
process (continuous EMA updates consuming internal-state signals), its
ablation is clean (skip the correction phase), and it couples with
homeostasis (modifying internal state) and response-to-stimuli (internal
state feeds the neural controller).  The additional condition for
Candidate~A is \textbf{experience-dependence}: performance should improve
as a function of within-lifetime experience under stationary genetics.

The \textbf{sham control} replaces the EMA trace with independent uniform
random draws each timestep, preserving the correction code path and
computational cost while destroying temporal coherence
\citep{floreano_2000_evolutionary, soltoggio_2018_born}.

\subsection{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:results} summarizes outcomes across all experiment suites.

\subsubsection*{Normal conditions (no perturbation)}
Survival AUC shows no significant effect of memory: enabled vs.\
baseline $d_z = 0.086$ (95\% CI $[-0.29, 0.46]$, $p_\mathrm{adj} =
1.00$).  TOST confirms equivalence ($p_\mathrm{TOST} = 0.015$).
Ablated and sham conditions are similarly indistinguishable
($|d_z| < 0.07$, both TOST $p < 0.02$).  Extinction: 0\% across all
conditions.

\subsubsection*{Famine stress}
Post-shock AUC: $d_z = 0.040$ (CI $[-0.33, 0.41]$, $p_\mathrm{adj} =
1.00$, TOST $p = 0.009$).  Extinction: baseline 86.7\%, enabled 93.3\%.
Memory provides no survival advantage under acute resource deprivation.

\subsubsection*{Boom-bust stress}
Survival AUC: $d_z = -0.193$ (CI $[-0.57, 0.18]$, $p_\mathrm{adj} =
0.35$, TOST $p = 0.052$).  The negative trend---sham and ablated
conditions trending better than enabled ($d_z = -0.32$ for sham)---suggests
that memory correction may \emph{destabilize} homeostasis under cyclic
stress, though no comparison reaches significance.

\subsection{Mechanism Verification}

Despite the null survival effect, the memory mechanism operates correctly
(Figure~\ref{fig:ema}).  The EMA late variance (variance of memory trace
across the final 5{,}000 timesteps) is significantly lower for the
enabled condition ($\bar{\sigma}^2 = 1.04 \times 10^{-4}$) than for the
sham control ($\bar{\sigma}^2 = 7.29 \times 10^{-4}$; Mann--Whitney
$U = 33$, $p < 10^{-9}$).  Under stress regimes, memory variance
\emph{increases} relative to sham (3$\times$ higher under famine),
indicating that the EMA tracks environmental turbulence faithfully but
this tracking destabilizes the correction signal.

The distinction between ``mechanism works'' and ``mechanism helps'' is
critical: the memory faithfully encodes experience, but the
evolutionary landscape under these perturbation regimes does not select
for memory-contingent behaviors.

\section{Candidate B: Collective Kin-Sensing}

\subsection{Implementation}

Inspired by Hamilton's inclusive fitness theory
\citep{hamilton_1964_genetical, west_2007_social}, Candidate~B adds a
\textbf{kin-sensing channel} that provides each agent with information
about nearby relatives.  A single-pass neighbor count partitions nearby
agents into kin (same genome lineage) and non-kin, computing a
\texttt{kin\_fraction} $\in [0, 1]$ that enters the neural controller
as an additional input (channel~8, expanding the input from 8 to 9
dimensions; 228 total weights).

This satisfies the functional analogy: it is a dynamic process
(recomputed each timestep from local spatial information), its ablation
is clean (fix \texttt{kin\_fraction} = 0), and it couples with
cellular organization (spatial structure determines kin encounters) and
reproduction (kin density reflects reproductive success).  The
additional condition for Candidate~B is \textbf{collective signal
utilization}: organisms with access to kin information should exhibit
measurably different collective behavior.

The \textbf{sham control} permutes real \texttt{kin\_fraction} values
across alive agents each timestep, preserving the population-level
distribution while destroying spatial correlation.

\subsection{Results}

\subsubsection*{Famine stress}
Post-shock AUC: $d_z = -0.141$ (CI $[-0.52, 0.23]$, $p_\mathrm{adj} =
0.64$, TOST $p = 0.030$).  Extinction: 96.7\% enabled vs.\ 86.7\%
baseline.

\subsubsection*{Boom-bust stress}
Survival AUC: $d_z = -0.065$ (CI $[-0.44, 0.31]$, $p_\mathrm{adj} =
1.00$, TOST $p = 0.012$).  Extinction: 76.7\% enabled vs.\ 83.3\%
baseline.  All conditions statistically equivalent.

\subsubsection*{Pre-shock window analysis}
To test whether Candidate~B provides benefit before demographic collapse
eliminates the kin signal, we analyzed the pre-shock phase (steps
0--3{,}000) separately.  During this window, organisms maintain larger
swarms (kin fraction $\sim$0.33), yet the effect remains null: $d_z =
0.22$ (famine, $p_\mathrm{adj} = 0.59$), $d_z = 0.31$ (boom-bust,
$p_\mathrm{adj} = 0.33$).

\subsection{Signal Sparsity Diagnosis}

The null result for Candidate~B has a clear mechanistic explanation
(Figure~\ref{fig:kin}): \textbf{organisms converge to approximately one
agent each} under the population cap ($\texttt{max\_alive\_organisms} =
100$).  When organisms consist of a single agent, \texttt{kin\_fraction}
$\approx 0$.  Mean \texttt{kin\_fraction} at simulation end: 0.03 under
famine, 0.19 under boom-bust (driven by rare multi-agent survivors).

This is an \emph{observability failure}: the kin signal exists in
principle but becomes degenerate under the tested demographic regime.

\section{Legitimacy Tests}

Beyond causal necessity (ablation), we conducted exploratory
orthogonality and non-reducibility analyses using existing experiment
data.

\subsection{Orthogonality: Incremental Predictive Validity}

We extracted seven proxy features from the late simulation window
(energy, waste, boundary integrity, genome diversity, spatial cohesion,
generation depth, genome drift) and fit OLS regressions predicting
survival AUC.  Adding the 8th-candidate binary indicator produced
negligible improvement:

\begin{itemize}
\item Candidate~A: $\Delta R^2 = 0.0008$ ($F = 0.83$, $p = 0.36$;
  permutation $p = 0.37$; CV $\Delta R^2 = -0.001$)
\item Candidate~B: $\Delta R^2 = 0.0008$ ($F = 0.39$, $p = 0.53$;
  permutation $p = 0.53$; CV $\Delta R^2 = -0.007$)
\end{itemize}

Bootstrap 95\% CIs for $\Delta R^2$ span $[0, 0.008]$ (Candidate~A)
and $[0, 0.012]$ (Candidate~B).  Cross-validated $\Delta R^2$ is
negative for both, indicating the candidate indicator adds noise
rather than signal.

\subsection{Non-Reducibility}

We tested whether each candidate's characteristic signal can be
predicted from the seven-criteria proxies alone.  High cross-validated
$R^2$ indicates the candidate signal is \emph{reducible} (predictable
from existing criteria):

\begin{itemize}
\item Candidate~A (memory late-window variance): CV $R^2 = 0.91$
  (permutation $p < 0.001$).  Interpretation: \textbf{reducible}.
\item Candidate~B (kin\_fraction): CV $R^2 = 0.88$ (permutation
  $p < 0.001$).  Interpretation: \textbf{reducible}.
\end{itemize}

Both candidate signals are almost entirely explained by existing
criteria.  This does not invalidate the candidates \emph{a priori}
(a successful candidate might still be reducible in signal but
non-reducible in survival benefit), but it confirms that neither
introduces genuinely novel information under the tested regimes.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Interpreting Double-Null Results}

Both candidates yield null results across all tested regimes.
Table~\ref{tab:results} shows that all paired effects are small:
the largest is $|d_z| = 0.32$ (Candidate~A, boom-bust, sham;
$p_\mathrm{adj} = 0.19$).  TOST equivalence testing confirms that 7
of 12 primary comparisons fall formally within our SESOI of $\pm 0.5$
($p_\mathrm{TOST} < 0.05$); the remaining 5 have TOST $p$ between
0.05 and 0.17, reflecting the inherent power limitation of $n = 30$.
Figure~\ref{fig:forest} visualizes the forest plot of all effect sizes
with SESOI bounds.

Importantly, these are \emph{diagnosed} nulls.  Each candidate fails
for a different reason:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Candidate~A} (Memory): The mechanism works---EMA converges
  correctly and encodes sensory history---but the evolutionary landscape
  does not select for memory-contingent behaviors.  Under stress,
  memory actually \emph{destabilizes} homeostasis (memory variance
  3$\times$ higher than sham under famine).
\item \textbf{Candidate~B} (Kin-Sensing): Demographic convergence to
  single-agent organisms eliminates the kin signal before it can be
  utilized.  Even in the pre-shock window where kin fraction is viable
  ($\sim$0.33), no survival benefit is observed.
\end{itemize}

The legitimacy tests strengthen these conclusions: neither candidate adds
incremental predictive validity ($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$), and both
candidate signals are reducible to existing criteria (CV $R^2 > 0.88$).

\subsection{Candidate Space}

Our two candidates represent distinct branches of a broader taxonomy of
possible eighth criteria (Figure~\ref{fig:taxonomy}): \emph{within-lifetime
adaptation} (Candidate~A) and \emph{collective organization}
(Candidate~B).  A third category---\emph{anticipatory regulation}
(e.g., predictive coding, immune-like recognition)---remains untested.
Other candidates within each category (synaptic plasticity, niche
construction) may succeed where our specific implementations did not.

\subsection{Implications for Multi-Scale ALife Design}

\textbf{Signal viability precedes signal utility.}  Candidate~B
demonstrates that a theoretically well-motivated mechanism can fail not
because of its logic but because ecological dynamics eliminate the
conditions under which the signal carries information.

\textbf{Perturbation structure matters for learning.}  Candidate~A's
memory accumulates information, but the famine regime offers no
within-lifetime gradient to learn from---it is a single catastrophic
event.  Future tests should include perturbation regimes with
\emph{repeated, learnable structure} (e.g., predictable seasonal cycles).

\textbf{Mechanism verification is essential.}  Without the EMA
convergence test and kin-fraction analysis, the null results would be
uninterpretable---they could indicate either ``the mechanism doesn't
help'' or ``the mechanism doesn't work.''


\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Regime specificity.}  Our two perturbation regimes (famine,
boom-bust) represent acute environmental stress.  Candidates may succeed
under seasonal cycles, spatial heterogeneity, or gradual perturbations.

\textbf{Population caps.}  The cap
($\texttt{max\_alive\_organisms} = 100$) drives convergence to
minimal-size organisms.  This is a compute constraint (M2 Pro hardware),
not a theoretical necessity.

\textbf{Controller capacity.}  The fixed-topology neural controller
($8/9 \to 16 \to 4$) may lack capacity to exploit memory or kin
information.  Topology evolution
\citep{stanley_2002_evolving} could change the outcome.

\textbf{Seven-criteria data at 2{,}000 steps.}  The 7-criteria
validation uses 2{,}000-step runs (vs.\ 10{,}000 for 8th-criterion
experiments).  All effects are extremely large ($d > 0.78$), so
the shorter horizon is unlikely to affect conclusions.

\textbf{Two candidates, one system.}  Different substrate architectures
may support different candidate mechanisms more naturally.

\textbf{Legitimacy test limitations.}  Our orthogonality and
non-reducibility analyses are exploratory (no pre-registration).
The proxy features may not fully capture each criterion's contribution,
and the regression approach assumes linear relationships.


\subsection{What Would Count as Success}

A successful candidate eighth criterion must meet:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Effect size}: $|d_z| \geq 0.5$ on survival AUC for
  enabled vs.\ baseline, sustained across $\geq 2$ perturbation regimes.
\item \textbf{Ablation}: Significant degradation ($p_\mathrm{adj}
  < 0.05$) when ablated.
\item \textbf{Sham inequality}: Enabled outperforms sham control.
\item \textbf{Orthogonality}: Significant $\Delta R^2$ ($p < 0.05$)
  beyond seven-criteria proxies.
\item \textbf{Non-reducibility}: Candidate signal poorly predicted
  from existing criteria (CV $R^2 < 0.5$).
\item \textbf{Mechanism verification}: Quantitative evidence that the
  mechanism operates as designed.
\end{enumerate}


%% ================================================================
\section{Conclusion}

We presented a falsifiable framework for evaluating candidate eighth
criteria of life-likeness, incorporating paired statistical analysis,
formal equivalence testing, and three legitimacy tests.  Validating the
framework against all seven existing criteria ($d = 0.78$--$14.7$,
all significant), we tested two candidates across two perturbation
regimes.  Both yield bounded null results: all paired $|d_z| < 0.33$,
with TOST confirming formal equivalence for the majority of comparisons.
Exploratory legitimacy tests confirm neither candidate adds predictive
power or introduces non-reducible information.

The contribution is fourfold: (1)~the evaluation protocol is reusable;
(2)~the 7-criteria validation demonstrates the system's diagnostic
power; (3)~the diagnosed nulls constrain the design space for future
candidates; and (4)~by reporting nulls explicitly, we contribute to
the cumulative record.

The search for what, if anything, lies beyond the textbook seven criteria
remains open.  Our framework provides a systematic, falsifiable approach
to that search.
